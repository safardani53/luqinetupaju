    03 Apr 2024, 00:00

    CAR-hire firms have been caught piling extra charges on customers at rental desks in foreign airports.

    An undercover investigation revealed companies are pressuring drivers into buying excess insurance even if they already have cover.

    And deposits worth £1,200 are being blocked from customers' credit cards, according to the investigation by MoneyMail.

    Inflated fees for petrol and diesels were uncovered at Goldcar while several firms were found ramping prices for hiring at the airport rather than booking online.

    Europcar, Goldcar and Firefly, which is owned by Hertz, were all visited during the mystery shopper research at Malaga Airport.

    Thrifty, which is also owned by Hertz, and Budget quoted hugely inflated costs for renting a car on the day, the investigation found.

    Drivers who damage cars abroad have to pay an excess for repairs - normally a few hundred pounds - although holiday destinations have hiked excess charges to closer to £1,000.

    Many motorists choose to take out excess waiver insurance with a third party ahead of the trip to avoid this payout.

    But there's no law to make hire firms accept these cheaper policies leaving staff free to force customers to either buy the firm's own waiver cover or pay a hefty deposit.

    Motorists then have to claim any charges back through their party insurer AFTER the holiday.

    Martyn James, of complaints website Resolver, said: "Car hire companies have got away with fleecing customers for decades. It’s about time the authorities clamped down on these sharp practices."

    Firms defended the charges and Firefly said it was "standard practice in the industry" to block an amount as a deposit while Goldcar apologised if all the insurance options had not been explained clearly.

    Not checking your rental car for damage could land you with a £2,000 surprise bill at the end of your holiday

    Europcar also apologised for piling on charges for an exchange rate discrepancy.

    The rip-off comes just days after it was revealed drivers are being hit by misleading repair practices pushing up the cost of insurance and holiday car hire.

    Avis, Budget, Hertz and Enterprise, which owns Alamo and National, admitted yesterday they didn't always fix minor damage that they'd charged drivers to repair.

    Instead, they claim to accept a lower price when they sell the vehicle.

    At the same time another investigation found UK insurers are routinely hiking repair costs by as much as double and pocketing cashback from garages for the difference.

    Insurers representing drivers involved in accidents that were not their fault are raising prices to make money from rival insurers who represent the at-fault driver.

    Experts said the practice was costing motorists up to £750 million, the equivalent of about five per cent on the average car insurance premium.
